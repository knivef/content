# Skyscraper Blog Writing Guide

This is the reference document for the skyscraper-blog skill. It is the authoritative source on research standards, post structure, voice requirements, the AI pattern audit, Google quality standards, formatting rules, and post-publish outreach.

---

## What a Skyscraper Post Is

A skyscraper blog post is not a longer version of what already exists.

It is the most useful, specific, and credible resource on a given topic -- written for a real audience, not a search engine.

Every post must pass three tests:

1. Would someone bookmark or share this?
2. Does it demonstrate first-hand knowledge or original thinking?
3. Does it read like a person wrote it?

## Pre-Writing: Research Phase

Before writing a single word, complete this checklist.

### Identify the Target Content

- Find 3 to 5 existing articles ranking for your target keyword
- Use a backlink tool (Ahrefs, Semrush) to confirm they have real referring domains
- Aim for content with at least 50 to 100 referring domains from reputable sites
- Confirm the topic is relevant to your audience and within your genuine expertise

### Audit the Existing Content

For each competitor article, answer these questions:

- What does it cover well?
- What is outdated, missing, or shallow?
- Does it offer original data, examples, or perspective -- or just summarize others?
- Is it generic and could have been written by anyone?

Document your answers. This becomes your "improvement brief."

### Define Your Angle

Pick one improvement vector before writing:

| Vector | What It Means |
|---|---|
| Original data | You have stats, a case study, or survey data no one else has |
| Deeper expertise | You have first-hand experience with the topic |
| New perspective | You have a contrarian or nuanced take that challenges the consensus |
| Updated accuracy | The existing content is outdated and you can add current information |
| Better structure | Existing content is comprehensive but poorly organized or hard to use |

Do not pick "longer." Length is not an improvement vector.

## Writing: Structure and Content Standards

### Opening

- Lead with the most useful insight, not a preamble
- State what the reader will get from this post within the first 3 sentences
- Do not open with "In today's world," "In this article, we will explore," or any variant
- Do not open with a question that the rest of the article promises to answer

### Body

Build the post around a clear logical structure. Every section must:

- Advance a specific argument or answer a specific question
- Contain at least one concrete example, data point, or real-world reference
- Avoid summarizing what other articles already say without adding to it

Section checklist:

- [ ] Does this section add something the competitor articles do not have?
- [ ] Is there at least one specific, verifiable fact or example?
- [ ] Is the most important information at the top of the section?
- [ ] Is there any filler that could be cut without losing meaning?

### Conclusion

- Do not use "In conclusion" or "In summary"
- End with a clear action, a direct recommendation, or a specific next step
- If you have a contrarian point, the conclusion is a good place to land it

## Tone and Voice Standards

### Write Like a Person

Every section must sound like it was written by someone with an actual opinion and real experience. That means:

- Use "you" and "your" when addressing the reader directly
- Include at least one specific example or anecdote per major section
- Take a clear stance. Do not hedge every claim with "it depends" or "some argue"
- Vary sentence length. Short sentences create emphasis. Longer sentences build context and carry more nuance. Mix them deliberately.

### Paragraph and Sentence Rhythm

- No two consecutive paragraphs should have the same structure
- Short paragraphs (1 to 3 sentences) for emphasis
- Longer paragraphs (4 to 6 sentences) for explanation or argument
- Never write three consecutive sentences of the same length

### Active Voice

Write active, not passive.

Wrong: "This approach is considered effective by many marketers."
Right: "Most marketers who test this approach see results within 60 days."

## AI Pattern Audit: Phrases and Structures to Remove

Before submitting any draft, run it through this audit. Flag and revise every instance.

### Inflated Meaning -- Cut These

- "stands as a testament to"
- "plays a vital / significant role"
- "watershed moment"
- "key turning point"
- "deeply rooted"
- "solidifies"
- "underscores its importance"
- "leaves a lasting impact"
- "captures the essence"

### Promotional Filler -- Cut These

- "rich heritage"
- "breathtaking"
- "enduring legacy"
- "rich tapestry"
- "must-see / must-visit"
- "stunning"

### Editorial Commentary -- Cut These

- "it's important to note"
- "it is worth mentioning"
- "no discussion would be complete without"
- "needless to say"
- "it goes without saying"

### Over-Used Connectors -- Limit Strictly

Use each of these a maximum of once per post:

- "moreover"
- "furthermore"
- "in addition"
- "in contrast"
- "on the other hand"

Replace them with direct sentence construction. If two ideas belong together, connect them with a comma, a colon, or by restructuring the paragraph.

### Negation Structures -- Rewrite These

The pattern: "It's not just X, it's Y."

This construction is the most common AI tell in marketing copy. Find every instance and rewrite it as a direct, positive claim.

AI pattern: "It's not just about driving traffic -- it's about building trust."
Rewrite: "The goal is trust, and traffic follows from that."

### Vague Authority -- Replace With Specific Sources

- "Industry reports suggest..."
- "Observers have noted..."
- "Some experts argue..."
- "Studies show..."

If you cannot name the report, the observer, the expert, or the study -- cut the claim or find a real source.

### Empty -ing Endings -- Remove These

Sentences that end with a vague participial phrase add no information:

- "...improving efficiency."
- "...ensuring scalability."
- "...highlighting the importance."
- "...reflecting the complexity."

Cut the -ing phrase. If the sentence cannot stand without it, the underlying claim is probably empty.

### Bullet Points with Bold Labels

This pattern is a strong AI signal:

> **Scalability:** The system is designed to scale easily.

Rewrite as prose or remove the bold label entirely. Use bullet points for genuinely list-like content, not to structure paragraphs.

## Google Quality Checklist: Before Publishing

Run this before every post goes live.

### Content Quality

- [ ] Does the post provide original information, analysis, or perspective not found in the top 5 competing articles?
- [ ] Is the most useful content placed at the top, not buried after a long introduction?
- [ ] Does every factual claim have a named, verifiable source?
- [ ] Is the title accurate and descriptive? Does it avoid exaggeration or shock framing?
- [ ] Would you bookmark or share this article?

### E-E-A-T Signals

- [ ] Is there a clear byline with author context?
- [ ] Does the post demonstrate first-hand experience with the topic (used the tool, ran the test, visited the place, shipped the product)?
- [ ] Does the post cite authoritative sources where claims go beyond the author's direct experience?
- [ ] For YMYL topics (health, finance, safety): are all claims supported by recognized expert or institutional sources?

### People-First Test

- [ ] Was this post written to genuinely help a specific audience -- not to rank for a keyword?
- [ ] After reading, will someone have enough information to act without needing to search again?
- [ ] Does the post avoid answering questions that have no real answer?

### Anti-Filler Test

- [ ] Is every paragraph earning its place?
- [ ] Are there any sections that exist only to add word count?
- [ ] Does the post answer the reader's question and then stop -- rather than padding toward an arbitrary length?

## Formatting Rules

### Headings

- Use H2 for major sections
- Use H3 for sub-points within a section
- Every heading should tell the reader what they are about to learn -- not tease it

### Bullet Points

- Use bullet points for genuine lists (steps, options, examples)
- Do not use bullets to break up what should be a paragraph
- Limit bullet lists to 5 to 7 items. Longer lists usually need grouping or prose

### Bold Text

- Bold only the most critical term or phrase in a section -- one per section maximum
- Do not bold every sub-point label in a bullet list
- Do not bold phrases that are immediately explained in the same sentence

### Tables

- Use tables for comparisons, criteria lists, or structured data
- Do not use tables for content that reads naturally as prose

## Outreach Brief (Post-Publishing)

Once the post is live, prepare a short outreach brief:

1. List the 3 to 5 posts your content improves upon
2. Pull the referring domains for each post using a backlink tool
3. For each prospect, identify:
   - The specific section in their article that links to the weaker resource
   - What your post adds that the original does not
4. Write a personalized outreach email per prospect -- not a template
   - Reference something specific about their article
   - State clearly what your post adds, not just that it is "better"
   - Keep it under 100 words
   - Include a direct link and suggest where the swap would fit

## What This Skill Does Not Produce

- Content written primarily to rank for a keyword with no real audience intent behind it
- Lists of tips assembled from other articles without original commentary
- Posts that answer a question vaguely and then ask the reader to contact you for the real answer
- Content that uses AI output as a final draft without a full human edit pass
