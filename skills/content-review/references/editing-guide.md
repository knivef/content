# Content Review Editing Guide

This is the reference document for the content-review skill. It is the authoritative source on structural editing, Google quality standards, the AI pattern audit, line-level editing rules, and the final publication checklist.

---

## What This Skill Produces

A piece of content that passes this guide's checklist should be one a reader would bookmark, share, or cite. The goal is not grammatical cleanliness. It is making content genuinely useful, credibly human, and worth reading.

---

## Pass 1: Structural Edit

Read the full post before making any edits. Do not correct individual sentences yet. You are evaluating the architecture.

### Questions to Answer

- What is the central argument or takeaway of this post?
- Is that argument clear within the first 3 sentences?
- Does each section advance the argument, or does it repeat or stall it?
- Is the most important information at the top of each section?
- Does the conclusion land on a specific point, action, or recommendation?

### Structural Problems to Fix

**Buried lede**

The main point appears halfway through the post or at the end. Move it to the top. Readers and Google's quality raters both penalize content that makes you work to find the answer.

**Padding sections**

A section exists to add word count, not to add meaning. Cut it entirely or merge it into an adjacent section.

**Redundant coverage**

Two sections cover the same ground with different headings. Merge or cut one.

**Weak opening**

The post opens with a preamble, a promise, or a rhetorical question. Rewrite the opening to lead with the most useful point.

Flag these openers for rewrite:

- "In today's world..."
- "Have you ever wondered..."
- "In this post, we will cover..."
- "As [topic] continues to grow..."
- Any sentence that describes what the article is about instead of making a point

**Weak conclusion**

The post ends with a summary of what was just covered, or trails off. Rewrite to end on a specific recommendation, direct action, or a clear point of view.

Flag these closers for rewrite:

- "In conclusion..."
- "In summary..."
- "As we have seen..."
- "We hope this article helped you..."

## Pass 2: Google Quality Edit

This pass checks the content against Google's helpful content standards. Any post that fails these checks is not ready to publish, regardless of how well it reads.

### Content Quality Checks

- [ ] Does the post provide original information, analysis, or perspective?

  If the post is primarily a summary of what other sources already say, flag it. Either add original commentary, first-hand experience, or a specific example that is not found in the source material. A post that adds no new information is a low-quality page by Google's definition.

- [ ] Does every factual claim have a named, verifiable source?

  Vague attribution ("studies show," "experts agree," "research suggests") is both an AI tell and a quality failure. Every claim that goes beyond the author's direct experience needs a named source with a link. If the source cannot be named, cut the claim.

- [ ] Is the most useful content placed at the top of each section?

  Supporting context, background, and caveats belong below the main point -- not above it.

- [ ] Does the title accurately describe the content?

  Flag titles that are exaggerated, misleading, or promise more than the content delivers.

- [ ] Would a reader finish this post and know what to do next?

  If the post leaves the reader needing to search again for the real answer, identify which section is failing and rewrite it with a specific recommendation or concrete next step.

### E-E-A-T Checks

- [ ] Is there a byline, and does it link to author context?

  Anonymous content scores lower on trust. If there is no byline, flag it. If the byline exists but links to nothing, flag it.

- [ ] Does the post demonstrate first-hand experience?

  Look for: specific examples the author has lived, products they have used, outcomes they have measured. Generic advice that could apply to anyone is a weak E-E-A-T signal. Flag sections that lack any grounding in direct experience.

- [ ] For YMYL topics: are claims supported by authoritative sources?

  If the post covers health, finance, legal, or safety topics, every major claim needs a named institutional or expert source. Flag and escalate any YMYL content that does not meet this standard.

### People-First Test

Ask this question about the post as a whole: was this written to help a specific reader -- or to rank for a keyword?

Signs it was written for rankings, not readers:

- Covers a topic the author has no real experience with
- Answers the question vaguely and pushes the reader toward a contact form
- Adds sections that are topically adjacent but not genuinely useful
- Promises to answer a question that has no real answer (release dates, predictions stated as fact)
- Changes or misrepresents dates to appear more current than the content actually is

Flag any of these patterns before the post is published.

## Pass 3: AI Pattern Edit

This pass removes the language patterns that signal AI-generated content. Run it on every post, regardless of whether AI was used. These patterns also appear in human writing that has absorbed AI conventions, and they all weaken the content.

Work through the post section by section. Flag every instance. Do not just skim for the words -- read each sentence in context.

### Pattern 1: Inflated Meaning

These phrases exaggerate the significance of ordinary statements. They add rhetorical weight without adding information.

Cut or rewrite every instance of:

- "stands as a testament to"
- "plays a vital / significant role"
- "watershed moment"
- "key turning point"
- "deeply rooted"
- "solidifies"
- "underscores its importance"
- "leaves a lasting impact"
- "captures the essence of"
- "continues to captivate"
- "marks a pivotal moment"
- "represents a significant shift"

How to fix: Remove the phrase entirely. If the underlying claim is real, state it plainly. If removing the phrase leaves nothing, the sentence was empty and should be cut.

### Pattern 2: Promotional Tone

These phrases create a sales or tourism copy register. They do not belong in factual or analytical content.

Cut or rewrite every instance of:

- "rich cultural heritage / rich history"
- "breathtaking"
- "must-visit / must-see"
- "stunning natural beauty"
- "enduring / lasting legacy"
- "rich tapestry"
- "world-class"

How to fix: Replace with a specific, verifiable description. "Rich history" tells the reader nothing. "Founded in 1847, the institution has trained over 40,000 practitioners" tells them something real.

### Pattern 3: Unnecessary Editorial Commentary

These phrases announce that something is important instead of demonstrating it.

Cut every instance of:

- "it's important to note / remember / consider"
- "it is worth mentioning"
- "no discussion would be complete without"
- "needless to say"
- "it goes without saying"
- "notably"
- "interestingly"
- "of course"

How to fix: Delete the phrase. The sentence almost always works better without it.

### Pattern 4: Overused Transition Phrases

These connectors are not wrong in isolation. The problem is frequency. AI output uses them as structural scaffolding, creating a predictable, mechanical rhythm.

Flag and limit to one use per post:

- "moreover"
- "furthermore"
- "in addition"
- "in contrast"
- "on the other hand"
- "additionally"

How to fix: Remove the connector and restructure the sentence. If two ideas belong together, connect them directly. If they do not belong together, they should be in separate paragraphs without a connector.

### Pattern 5: Negation Structures

This is the most common AI pattern in marketing and business writing.

The structure: "It's not just X -- it's Y." Or across two sentences: "X is one way to think about it. But Y is the real picture."

How to identify: Search for "not just," "not only," "but rather," "it's not about," "rather than simply." Read the surrounding context.

How to fix: State the positive claim directly.

- AI pattern: "It's not just about driving traffic -- it's about building trust."
- Rewrite: "The real goal is trust. Traffic follows."

### Pattern 6: Vague Authority

These phrases suggest evidence without providing it. They are a quality failure and an AI tell simultaneously.

Flag every instance of:

- "industry reports suggest..."
- "observers have noted..."
- "some critics argue..."
- "studies show..."
- "experts agree..."
- "research indicates..."
- "many believe..."

How to fix: Name the report, the observer, the study, the expert. If you cannot name it, cut the claim.

### Pattern 7: Empty -ing Endings

Sentences that end with a vague participial phrase pretend to add meaning without delivering it.

Examples to flag:

- "...improving customer experience."
- "...ensuring long-term scalability."
- "...highlighting the importance of trust."
- "...reflecting the complexity of the issue."
- "...driving sustainable growth."

How to fix: Cut the -ing phrase. If the sentence cannot stand without it, rewrite with a specific outcome or replace with a concrete example.

### Pattern 8: Bold Label Bullet Points

The pattern to flag:

> **Efficiency:** The system is designed to process requests faster.

The bold label restates what the sentence already says.

How to fix: Remove the bold label. Either let the sentence stand on its own in the list, or rewrite as prose if the content does not actually need to be a list.

### Pattern 9: Uniform Sentence Structure

Read two consecutive paragraphs aloud. If they have the same rhythm -- same average sentence length, same structure -- flag it.

How to fix: Introduce deliberate variation. Break a long sentence into two short ones. Merge two short sentences into one. Add a single-sentence paragraph where the content calls for emphasis.

### Pattern 10: No Specificity, No Texture

Signs of this pattern:

- No named people, companies, products, or places
- No numbers, dates, or measurable outcomes
- Every example is hypothetical ("imagine a company that...")
- No point of view -- the post presents all sides without taking any position
- The advice could apply to anyone in any situation

How to fix: For each major section, ask: what specific example, data point, or direct experience grounds this claim? If the answer is nothing, the section needs a real anchor before it is publishable.

## Pass 4: Line-Level Edit

This pass works sentence by sentence. It runs after the structural, quality, and AI pattern passes are complete.

### Cut Filler Words

Remove on sight:

- "very," "quite," "rather," "somewhat," "fairly"
- "basically," "essentially," "generally," "typically"
- "actually," "literally," "truly," "certainly"
- "just," "simply," "easily"

### Fix Passive Constructions

Find every instance of "is / are / was / were + past participle" and rewrite in active voice.

- Passive: "This approach is considered effective."
- Active: "Most teams that test this approach see results within 30 days."

### Tighten Long Sentences

Any sentence over 30 words should be examined. Split it or cut what is not essential.

### Remove Redundant Pairs

- "each and every" -- use "every"
- "first and foremost" -- use "first"
- "past history" -- use "history"
- "end result" -- use "result"
- "future plans" -- use "plans"
- "completely eliminate" -- use "eliminate"

### Check Pronoun Clarity

Every "it," "this," "they," and "these" should have an unambiguous referent in the previous sentence. If there is any doubt about what the pronoun refers to, replace it with the noun.

## Final Publication Checklist

Run this before approving any post for publication.

### Content

- [ ] The main point is clear within the first 3 sentences
- [ ] Every section adds something not in the top competing articles
- [ ] Every factual claim has a named, verifiable source
- [ ] The title is accurate and does not exaggerate
- [ ] The conclusion ends on a specific point, recommendation, or action
- [ ] No section exists only to add length

### E-E-A-T

- [ ] There is a byline with a link to author context
- [ ] The post demonstrates direct experience or expertise
- [ ] YMYL claims (if any) are backed by authoritative sources

### AI Pattern Clean

- [ ] No inflated meaning phrases
- [ ] No promotional filler
- [ ] No editorial commentary phrases
- [ ] No overused transition phrases beyond one per post
- [ ] No negation structures ("it's not X, it's Y")
- [ ] No vague authority attribution
- [ ] No empty -ing endings
- [ ] No bold label bullet points
- [ ] Sentence rhythm is varied throughout
- [ ] Every major section is anchored in a specific example, data point, or direct experience

### Line Level

- [ ] Filler words removed
- [ ] Passive constructions rewritten
- [ ] No sentences over 30 words that can be tightened
- [ ] Redundant pairs removed
- [ ] All pronouns have clear referents

---

## What This Skill Does Not Do

- It does not make content longer to hit a word count
- It does not add sections that are not genuinely useful to the reader
- It does not soften or hedge specific claims in the name of "balance"
- It does not preserve phrases that weaken the writing just because they were in the original draft
- It does not approve content for publication if E-E-A-T signals are missing
